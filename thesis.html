Solarized Histories: Archival Distortion as Afterlife and Redress in Lisa Oppenheim’s Spolia

This thesis examines Lisa Oppenheim’s Spolia (2024), a photographic meditation on Nazi cultural looting and the archival violence that continues to shape how looted Jewish artworks are remembered. Through techniques of solarization, repetition, and fragmentation, Oppenheim transforms bureaucratic Nazi records into spectral images that resist closure and critique the archive’s role in cultural erasure. Drawing on theories of hauntology, the uncanny, and ethical representation, the project situates Spolia as a counter-archive that reanimates absence without attempting restitution.
